# Representation at Vilnius University

Student representatives – Klėja Merčaitytė, Justinas Kondratas.

The VU Council is the University’s governing body, which oversees whether the University’s activities and management comply with the University’s mission, operational goals, objectives, and principles, as well as general academic interests and the requirements for transparency and accountability to the public as set forth in the Statute.

During the reporting period, eight VU Council meetings were held, along with several electronic votes. This year, the VU Council devoted significant attention to the development of the University’s strategy, ensuring that the plan included clear progress indicators, strategic goals, and the necessary resources for achieving those goals. In formulating the University’s long-term policy, the VU Council approved the VU Strategic Plan for 2026–2030, which includes outcomes important to students, such as the expansion and renovation of dormitories, a plan to enhance the quality of doctoral studies, encouraging student involvement in research activities, mentoring, and more.

At the initiative of the VU Council, discussions were organized with the heads of VU’s core academic units to address the most pressing issues at the University. The VU Council also considered issues regarding international students at the University, strategic international marketing directions, geographic priorities, and effectiveness evaluation, as well as the strategy for the development of doctoral studies. During these discussions, student representatives highlighted the need to strengthen the integration process for international students and to standardize admission rules across VU’s core academic units. In the discussion on improving the quality of doctoral studies, expectations were expressed to increase funding from the doctoral fund for doctoral students, and the importance of training for doctoral supervisors and the expansion of doctoral schools was emphasized. Attention was also drawn to the right to joint doctoral programs with other academic institutions and to ensure equal conditions for doctoral students.

During the reporting period, the VU Council approved the VU 2025 Activity Report, the VU Income and Expenditure Budget Execution Report for 2025, the structure of the 2026 VU budget and the description of the procedure for its preparation, as well as the 2026 University budget. Decisions were made regarding the remuneration policy for VU employees, the reduction and expansion of the VU campus boundaries, and the allocation of state budget appropriations for science (and the arts).

The VU Senate is the University’s governing body, composed of members of the VU academic community. The Senate is responsible for overseeing and improving the quality of studies, as well as fostering freedom of culture, science, research, and teaching at the University. The Senate’s work is divided into three main areas of activity. The Committees on Studies Affairs, Science Affairs, and Quality and Development Affairs examine issues relevant to their respective areas of activity and submit their conclusions to the VU Senate for approval.

During the reporting period (from the 2024–2025 academic year VU SR ARC to the 2025–2026 academic year VU SR ARC), 14 Senate meetings and 7 electronic votes took place. Student representatives also participated in one of the VU Senate’s strategic sessions, the formal ceremony for conferring the title of Honorary Doctor of Vilnius University, and a ceremonial Senate meeting. One of the Senate’s strategic sessions examined the processes for competitions for positions among Vilnius University faculty and research (art) staff, as well as performance evaluation and certification.

The most relevant issues discussed by the Senate for students:

- Review of the Vilnius University Plan for the Prevention of Academic Ethics Violations

In 2021 The VU SR Parliament approved the VU SR position on the prevention of academic ethics violations, which provided for the development of a plan to prevent academic ethics violations—since then, VU SR student representatives in the Senate have worked purposefully to ensure that this plan is created. In the first half of 2025, a Senate working group was formed to draft this plan. In March 2026, the University Senate approved the action plan and, through this document, established a consistent, evidence-based, and community-wide approach to the prevention of academic misconduct for the 2026–2028 period. The plan outlines the following priorities: (i) to strengthen the culture of academic ethics and the community’s trust in learning outcomes; (ii) to reduce the prevalence of academic dishonesty, including risks associated with the use of AI; (iii) to ensure clear, uniformly understood, and applied rules and procedures; (iv) to develop prevention measures focused on education, counseling, and the implementation of fair assessment practices; (v) to ensure continuous evaluation of the effectiveness of measures and accountability.

- Amendment to the procedure for concluding fixed-term employment contracts with faculty members and research (art) staff over the age of 65

According to Vilnius University regulations, research staff and lecturers who have reached the age of 65 must be approved by the VU Senate to continue their academic activities. This year, the Vilnius University Senate reviewed the procedure for concluding fixed-term employment contracts with lecturers and research (art) staff over the age of 65. At the initiative of VU SR student representatives, the procedure was amended to include the requirement to rely on surveys conducted in the VU information system; furthermore, it was established that student representatives from the VU SR would provide the students’ opinion on the lecturer, thereby further strengthening the students’ voice in the decision-making process regarding contract extensions.

## Model of the research promotion fund's operations

The Vilnius University research promotion fund is composed of state funds allocated to the University for research. The research promotion fund finances mobility activities for young researchers (e.g., trips to conferences), the publication of scientific works, the implementation of research ideas, and the costs of inviting guest lecturers. Not only researchers but also VU students are eligible for the latter two measures. During the reporting period, the issue of reorganizing the Research Promotion Fund was discussed. VU SR student representatives expressed a unanimous position that the fund’s purpose—to support young VU researchers—must remain unchanged, and that funding allocated for this purpose must not be reduced.

## Representation in Academic Units

During the reporting period, students’ opinions and voices were represented not only at the central level but also at the level of VU’s Core Academic Units (VU CAU)—student representatives served on the councils of all units and on the committees of various study programs.

The VU CAU Councils consider various matters: the VU CAU activity plan, budget, research directions, and the composition of the SPC and VU CAU committees (excluding student representatives) are approved; new study programs and changes to existing ones are discussed; and the results of research and studies are evaluated. The VU CAU Council consists of the head of VU CAU, faculty members, members of the academic and non-academic communities, social partners, and students.

Study Program Committees (SPCs) are academic bodies responsible for coordinating study programs and overseeing their implementation. SPCs ensure the consistency of study program objectives, competencies developed, content, methods, and student achievement assessments, and improve the study program by analyzing the opinions of students, faculty, graduates, social partners, and other indicators of the quality of study program implementation. An SPC consists of at least 3 faculty members, at least 1 social partner, and at least 1 student representative.

To ensure high-quality student representation throughout the year, training sessions were organized for student representatives to enhance their competencies in opinion-gathering methods, tools for representation, advocacy, and other related topics at both the central and unit levels.

## VU Central Dispute Resolution Commission

## Student Representatives – Vismantas Daujotas, Samanta Valiušaitytė, Eglė Lukauskaitė

The Commission reviews complaints regarding the legality and validity of decisions made by departmental dispute resolution commissions, as well as instances of inaction or failure to implement decisions. This provision does not apply to appeals regarding the final assessment of academic achievements. It also examines statements regarding disputes arising between students and Central Administration staff, provided such disputes relate to academic or study activities. Neither academic ethics nor work relations disputes fall under this category. In addition, the Commission examines other complaints related to academic and study activities that are not within the jurisdiction of departmental dispute resolution commissions or other University bodies. During the reporting period, three meetings were held, during which four complaints were examined.

## VU Central Electoral Commission

## Student Representatives – Akvilė Banytė, Vismantas Daujotas

The Central Electoral Commission of Vilnius University ensures a transparent and properly organized election process for the University’s governing bodies. The Commission organizes and conducts elections for members of the Senate (excluding student representatives) and the Council, as well as a public competition for the position of Rector. It registers all elected or appointed members of the Senate and the Council, determines the results of the Rector’s election, and approves the forms of election documents. The Commission’s functions also include assessing the eligibility of candidates, compiling lists of employees eligible to vote, approving election commissions of the core academic units, announcing election dates and procedures, and overseeing their organization. The Commission has the right to request additional information and documents from candidates or University units as needed for decision-making. When necessary, it submits proposals to the Senate regarding the improvement of documents regulating election procedures and ensures the quality of election organization.

During the reporting period, 14 meetings were held.

## WORK GROUPS

## Working Group (WG) on Improving the System of Teaching Competencies for Professors

Student Representative – Povilas Virbašius.

The European University Association (EUA) initiated thematic peer group on learning and teaching brings together representatives from selected universities each year to discuss practices in organizing learning and teaching at the institutional level and to identify examples of best practices. During the reporting period, Vilnius University participated in a thematic working group dedicated to examining the quality

and impact of staff professional development in the field of teaching.

The working group analyzed how staff development contributes to the quality of studies and the strengthening of the teaching culture at institutions, with a particular focus on impact assessment. The study examined how institutions can effectively use various data sources – including institutional surveys, peer and student feedback – to assess the impact of staff development activities on learning outcomes and the study experience. The aim was also to identify key indicators for assessing the effectiveness of these activities and their contribution to the achievement of institutional teaching and learning goals.

The working group’s outcome is a thematic report titled “Ensuring the quality and impact of staff development in teaching,” which offers insights into the links between staff development and the quality of education, identifies key challenges (e.g., the workload of lecturers, insufficient recognition of teaching activities, fragmented development initiatives), and provides recommendations for higher education institutions. These include integrating staff development activities into institutional strategies, systematically evaluating their impact, strengthening the recognition of teaching activities, and making data-driven decisions. Povilas Virbašius, as a student representative, participated in two working group meetings – in Portugal (at the University of Coimbra) and in Ireland (at the University of Limerick), and also contributed to integrating the student perspective into the working group’s discussions and final recommendations.
